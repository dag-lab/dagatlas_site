Causative Type

Causative constructions (see also глаголы с понудительным значением [Бокарев 1949Бокарев А. А. Очерк грамматики чамалинского языка. Издательство Академии наук СССР, 1949.], понудительный залог глагола [Сулейманов 1957Сулейманов Я. Г. Грамматический очерк андийского языка (по данным говора с. Риквани). М.: Институт языкознания Академии наук союза ССР, 1957.]) are widespread in the languages of the world, and in Dagestanian languages in particular. Causative construction presumes the existence of two situations, the cause and its result. The new agent called causer is added. Former subject (S or P of the initial situation), now the ‘causee’, gets lower syntactic position. The main parameters of the typological variation of causatives are, firstly, the ways to form the causative (morphologically or analytically) and, secondly, the case-marking of the causee.

East Caucasian languages include languages with the morphological causative (Andi, Bats, Chamalal, Chechen, Itsari, Tanti, Godoberi, Hunzib, Ingush, Lezgian, Tindi, Tsez, Udi) and languages with the analytical causative (Archi, Khinalug, Lak, Rutul, Tabasaran). For some languages with the morphological causative, analytic construction is also described in grammar (Aghul, Akhvakh, Avar, Bagvalal, Botlikh, Budukh, Hinuq, Karata, Khwarshi, Kubachi, Mehweb).

Morphological causative is formed by adding a special affix to the original verb. In (1), to form the verb meaning ‘wake up’, suffix k’ is added to the verb stem -arče, which means ‘get up’ (Hunzib, example from [Van den Berg 1995Van den Berg H. A Grammar of Hunzib (with Texts and Lexicon). München: Lincom Europa, 1995.]): -arče ‘get up’, -arče-k’ ‘wake up’.

(1) Hunzib
iyu-ly-arče-k’-erkid
mother-ERG2-get.up-CAUS-PRETgirl/2
‘mother woke up the daughter’

Sometimes it is said in grammar that causative from intransitive forms transitive, whereas causative from transitive provides causative actually (Karata, Tindin, Botlikh grammars). Analytical causative construction consists of the finite auxiliary verb and the infinitive of the lexical verb. Verbs most commonly used as causativizers are ‘do, make’ (e.g. Aghul), but also ‘guide’ (Akhvakh), ‘let’ (Khwarshi), ‘overcome’ (Kubachi). In (2) one can see the analytical causative of ‘see’ - ‘show’ (Khwarshi, example from [Khalilova 2009]). The auxiliary verb is ‘let’.

(2) Khwarshi, [Khalilova 2009Khalilova Z. A Grammar of Khwarshi. Utrecht: LOT, 2009.]
ut’ana-t’azidoro-bol-akw-al-ešt’-unboc’-i
red-PLmaterial.OBL-PL.ABSNHPL-see-INFNHPL-let-PST.UWwolf.OBL-ERG
‘The wolf showed the red material’.

The map is dedicated to the causative type:
(1) morphological;
(2) analytical;
(3) morphological and analytical.

Causee Marking

Causative constructions (see also глаголы с понудительным значением [Бокарев 1949Бокарев А. А. Очерк грамматики чамалинского языка. Издательство Академии наук СССР, 1949.], понудительный залог глагола [Сулейманов 1957Сулейманов Я. Г. Грамматический очерк андийского языка (по данным говора с. Риквани). М.: Институт языкознания Академии наук союза ССР, 1957.]) are widespread in the languages of the world, and in Dagestanian languages in particular. Causative construction presumes the existence of two situations, the cause and its result. The new agent called causer is added. Former subject (S or P of the initial situation), now the ‘causee’, gets lower syntactic position. The main parameters of the typological variation of causatives are, firstly, the ways to form the causative (morphologically or analytically) and, secondly, the case-marking of the causee.

The case of the causee depends on the initial verb. If it is intransitive, the causee takes the position of P and acquires nominative case. If it is a transitive verb, the positions of A and P are already occupied and the causee takes the next vacant slot in the syntactic hierarchy [Comrie 1989Comrie B. Language Universals and Linguistic Typology. Syntax and Morphology. Chicago: The University of Chicago Press, 1989]. Additionally, the choice of the case may depend on the semantic factors.

In Northeast Caucasian languages causee is often marked with locative cases (Aghul, Bagvalal, Bats, Godoberi, etc.) (1), sometimes with dative (Ingush, Lak, Udi) (2) or ergative (Aghul, Kubachi) (3).

(1) Causee is marked with contessive case (Godoberi, [Kibrik et al. 1996Kibrik A. E., Tatevosov S. G., Eulenberg A. Studies in Godoberi. Lincom Europa, 1996.])
a.waš-u-diizur-ali
son-OBL-ERGclothesNPL-put.on.PST
‘The son put on the clothes’.
b.il-u-diwaš-u-č’uizur-al-ali
mother-OBL-ERGson-OBL-CONTclothesNPL-put.on.CAUS.PST
‘Mother dressed her son in the clothes’.
(2) Causee is marked with dative case (Udi, [Майсак 2008Майсак Т.А. Глагольная парадигма удинского языка (ниджский диалект). Ганенков Д. С., Ландер Ю. А., Майсак Т.А. Удинский сборник: грамматика, лексика, история языка. М.: Academia, 2008.])
a.χüjär-ensijoʁ=eboχ-i
girl-ERGporridge=3SGcook-AOR
‘The girl cooked porridge’.
b.nana-nχüjär-äsijoʁ=eboχ-es-t:-i
mother-ERGgirl-DATporridge=3SGcook-INF-CAUS-AOR
‘The mother made the girl cook porridge’.
(3) Causee is marked with ergative case (Aghul, [Daniel et al. 2012Daniel M., Maisak T., Merdanova S. R. Causatives in Aghul. Argument structure and grammatical relations. A cross-linguistic typology / Ed. by P. Suihkonen, B. Comrie, V. Solovyev. Issue 126. Philadelphia, Amsterdam: John Benjamins Publishing Company, 2012. P. 55-113.])
baw.aruš.ajak:ʕut’.a-sq’.u-ne
mother(ERG)daughter(ERG)meat(NOM)eat.IPF-INFdo.PF-PFT
‘The mother made her daughter eat meat’.

The latter case is particularly interesting. In [Daniel et al. 2012Daniel M., Maisak T., Merdanova S. R. Causatives in Aghul. Argument structure and grammatical relations. A cross-linguistic typology / Ed. by P. Suihkonen, B. Comrie, V. Solovyev. Issue 126. Philadelphia, Amsterdam: John Benjamins Publishing Company, 2012. P. 55-113.] it is proposed that the analytical causative construction in Aghul is biclausal. Normally, two agentive participants in ergative cannot occur in one clause together. Acceptability of (3) indicates that the combination of the infinitive ‘eat’ and the verb ‘do’ appears to be a biclausal construction. It is worth noting, however, that apudessive causee marking in these sentences is preferable; then the construction is considered to be monoclausal (4).

(4) Aghul, [Daniel et al. 2012Daniel M., Maisak T., Merdanova S. R. Causatives in Aghul. Argument structure and grammatical relations. A cross-linguistic typology / Ed. by P. Suihkonen, B. Comrie, V. Solovyev. Issue 126. Philadelphia, Amsterdam: John Benjamins Publishing Company, 2012. P. 55-113.]
χalid.a[lik’.a-saq’.a-j-e]VPmegada.ji-w,meramazanʁ.agada.ji-w...
Khalid(ERG)write.IPF-INFdo.IPF-CVB-COPthisboy-APUDthisRamazan(NOM)say.IPFboy-APUD
‘Khalid forces this guy to write it, this guy called Ramazan’.

Fortis Ejectives

Fortis ejectives are pronounced glottalic egressive airstream and characterized by the intensiveness (tension) of the articulation [Kodzasov 1977]. In 16 languages of the sample fortis ejectives are not attested (59.26%). Fortis ejectives can be found mostly in the languages of the north-western part of the Daghestan. Languages demonstrating fortis ejectives are divided into two groups — «small» and «large». Languages of the «small» group have up to seven fortis ejectives, languages of the «large» group demonstrate from 8 to 11 fortis ejectives.

Gemination

Gemination is an audible lengthening of the consonants.

Twelve languages od the sample lack geminated consonants (44.44%). The rest of the languages of the sample was divided into three groups depending on the number of the geminated phonemes attested. Group marked on the map as «small» includes languages demonstrating up to 10 geminated phonemes. Group marked as «medium» on the map includes languages demonstrating from 15 to 25 geminates. Group «large» includes languages demonstrating from 30 to 44 geminates.

Labialization

Labialization is a secondary articulation involving the lips while producing another sound. This map describes 27 languages of East Caucasian language family.

There are seven languages of the sample lacking labialized vowels (25.93%). The rest of the languages in the sample demonstrates labialized consonants. Since the number of the components varies they were divided into three groups.

The first group is a group of languages with a small number of labialized consonants attested – up to 10 phonemes. These languages are marked as «small» on the map. Second group is a group with an average number of labialized consonants attested – from 13 to 26 phonemes. These languages are marked as «medium» on the map. Second group includes 9 languages. Five language of the sample demonstrate relatively large amount of labialized consonants – from 34 to 46 phonemes. These language form a third group of languages and marked as «large» on the map.

Laterals

Lateral consonants are produced by airstream proceeding along the side of the tongue. The most common laterals are approximants.

There are 16 languages demonstrating obstruent laterals in the sample (59.26% of the sample). These languages were divided into two groups. First group consists of the languages demonstrating from 4 to 6 laterals. First group is marked as «small» on the map. There are eleven languages in the sample demonstrating only lateral approximant /l/ (40.74% of the sample). These languages are also included in the group «small». Languages of the second group are marked on the map as «large» and demonstrate from 10 to 19 laterals.

Optative

Optative is a verbal category related to the expression of the wish of the speaker. According to [Dobrushina 2011], East Caucasian languages can distinguish between two types of optative: performative and desiderative. The former is used in blessing and curses and other kinds of ritual formulae. While it is not typical for languages of the world to have a dedicated form to express this meaning, most Caucasian languages have inflectional performative optative.

(1) Abkhaz
sɨ-ps-aːjt’
1SG-die-PFM.OPT
‘May I die!’

Pharyngealization

Pharyngealization is a secondary articulation of consonants and vowels involving constriction of the pharynx or epiglottis. For some languages, the status of the pharyngealized phonemes is still under discussion [Maddieson 2009]. There are four languages on the map that demonstrate pharyngealized consonants. They are Khwarshi, Rutul, Aghul and Tabassaran.

Preverbs

Preverbs are elements occurring before the verb root and adding specific meaning (prototypically locative and directive) to the verb base. It is a traditional notion for the description of languages of the Caucasus.

We do not treat negative elements and agreement slots, which can take place in prefixal position in verb too, as preverbs. In case if language has only such elements we treat it as a language without preverbs.

In some cases it is not obvious, does the language have preverbs or not. For instance, in Tsez preverbs, probably, could be attested before, but on the synchronic level they are bound up with the verbal root [Comrie 2007]. They are no longer regarded as independent morphemes. We decided to treat languages like Tsez as languages without preverbs. There are also cases when we use the term “non-productive preverbs” for describing a language system. For example, in Tsakh preverbal elements are attested because of the presence of agreement slot between the prefix and the root. Such elements can be treated as independent morphemes with zero meaning.

It is important to mention the relation of preverbs and postpositions (postpositional cases), prepositions and locative adverbs. In Chechen the one world can behave like adverbial, postposition or preverb [Jakovlev 1960]. In Batsbi most postpositions cognate to adverbs but also can be a part of the verb preceding its root.

(1) Batsbi [Desheriev 1953]
a.t’iv-enmakVa
bridge-datonis
‘he is on the bridge’
b.otta:–mak-ottă
stay–on-stay
'to stay'–'to step'

Furthermore, in Bats preverbs can have aspectual meaning. Probably, it has appeared under the influence of Georgian language. Among twelve preverbal elements attested in this language there are ones with complex meaning.

(2) Batsbi [Desheriev 1953]
ħalaħŏ
‘up and down’

Preverbs also can be morphologically complex. It means that there can be more than one preverb before the root. Usually, in this case there are two preverbs, where orientation preverb precedes the direction one. For instance, such clusters are typical for Dargwa.

(3) Dargwa
i-ka-b=iχ
onto-downwards-n=put
‘sit down on something’

As was mentioned above, preverbs can have not only locative or directive meaning. In Agul repetitive preverb is attested [Maisak et al. 2002]. In Ingush there is a reciprocal preverbal element [Nichols 2011].

Dialects of the same language can have slightly different preverbal systems. Usually, they have different number of preverbal elements or similar systems with the same functions, but with some phonetic differences.

Another fact in which we were interested in is the number of preverbs attested in each language with productive preverbal system. All the numbers were taken from the grammatical descriptions which sometimes lack reliable information on this point. The smallest number of preverbs was attested in Hunzib – there are 4 productive preverbs, and the biggest amount – in the dialects of Dargwa (>20 preverbs).

Voiced Uvular

Voiced uvular /ɢ/ is a rare phoneme comparing to other uvulars. Voiced uvular stop is attested in six languages of the sample (22.22%) — Andi, Khinalug, Budukh, Kryz, Rutul and Tsakhur.
